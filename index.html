Did you know?
That 22 veterans commit suicide every day.
That's 660 every month and 7920 every year.


We are ignoring the hard facts.
In 2008 a Rand Study was done to analyze the mental health of post deployed veterans. 1,965 veterans were surveyed.

14% of these veterans were diagnosed with Post Traumatic Stress Disorder, and 14% were diagnosed with Depression. Additionally, 9% of the veterans were diagnosed with both PTSD and depression.


About the book

These numbers are alarming, but what's even more alarming is the treatment of these veterans. The military base pharmacies, which the veterans use, do not stock the medications proven effective in treating both PTSD and depression. This book explains what PTSD is, how a veteran gets it, and what medications are effective in treating it. PTSD is treatable, but you need medications that have been proven effective. These medications are available, and it is necessary to start stocking them in the military base pharmacies.

It's outrageous that we are losing 22 veterans a day to suicide on our own soil because they are being denied proper mental health treatment. War is traumatic for all, and the V.A's health care system is not giving our soldiers the health care they deserve.

About the author

Herbert Wagemaker is a board-certified psychiatrist.

He has a lifelong passion for helping people who suffer from mental illness. He has extensively researched PTSD and successfully helped numerous people achieve and maintain functionality in their daily lives. In this book he shares crucial information that explains why some current treatment options just do not work and most importantly, which ones do.

Dr. Wagemaker taught at the University of Louisville from 1975 to 1986 during which time his research in the causes of schizophrenia was funded by the National Institute of Mental Health. He was also involved in psychopharmacology research. He later joined the faculty of the Department of Psychiatry at the University of Florida’s teaching hospital, Shands Jacksonville, where he was director of inpatient psychiatry.
